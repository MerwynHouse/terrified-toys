# Terrified Toys — Packaging Brief

## What's Confirmed vs. What Needs a Decision

**Researched directly, not assumed:** there is **no mandatory pet-toy-specific safety standard** in NZ or Australia. AS/NZS ISO 8124 (the toy safety standard most people have heard of) is **mandatory for children's toys only** (specifically toys for children up to 36 months for the small-parts/choking provisions) — it does not legally apply to pet toys, and pet toys currently have no equivalent mandatory certification or testing regime in NZ/AU. Some pet brands voluntarily test against it anyway as a quality signal. **This is a decision for Daniel, not a legal requirement:** voluntary AS/NZS ISO 8124-style testing adds cost and credibility; skipping it is legal but means no third-party safety signal on pack.

What **does** apply regardless: general consumer product safety and fair-trading law (NZ Fair Trading Act, Australian Consumer Law) — no misleading claims, genuinely safe materials, accurate description. See `terms-of-service.md` and `returns-refund-policy.md` for how this connects to consumer guarantees.

## Packaging Size & Format

Sized to the product, not a fixed standard — recommend:
- A **printed cardboard sleeve/header card** rather than a full box at this scale (10-25 unit demo run) — cheaper, faster to source in small quantities, and lets the plush itself (the actual selling point) be visible/touchable through a poly bag or clear window, which matters for an impulse-purchase gag-gift product.
- If selling online-only for the demo run (current plan, no physical retail yet), packaging needs to prioritize **shipping protection** over retail shelf appeal — a simple branded poly bag/mailer with a printed card insert is sufficient and avoids over-investing in retail-shelf packaging before any retail conversation exists (see `wholesale-strategy.md` for when that changes).
- **Revisit packaging format entirely once wholesale/retail is real** (per `growth-plan.md` Year 2+) — retail shelf presence has different requirements (barcode, shelf-readability, theft-deterrent considerations) than a DTC mailer.

## Materials

- Recommend **recycled/recyclable cardboard** for the header card or sleeve — fits general sustainability expectations without needing to make it a headline claim (no need to over-promise "100% sustainable" language that invites Fair Trading Act scrutiny if not literally true).
- Poly bag or compostable mailer for shipping protection — compostable mailers cost more per unit at small volume; a plain recyclable poly bag is a reasonable demo-run compromise, revisit at scale.

## What Must Appear on Packaging

**Legally/practically necessary:**
- Brand name and product name (the SKU — "The Deer," "The Rabbit," "The Possum")
- Materials/fabric content (general good practice, not strictly mandated for pet toys, but expected by consumers and protects against a misleading-claim issue if materials are misdescribed)
- Country of manufacture — once a manufacturer is confirmed (see `suppliers.md`), include accurate "Made in [country]" — getting this wrong is a Fair Trading Act/Australian Consumer Law issue, not just a style choice
- Care/safety instruction: **recommend a deliberate small-parts/supervision warning despite it not being legally mandated for pet toys** — e.g. *"Supervise play. Remove and discard if damaged. Not a children's toy — keep away from young children due to small parts."* This is honest (the whole product is designed to come apart) and reduces real risk regardless of what's strictly required.
- Contact details / website (terrified-toys.vercel.app, or successor domain)

**Brand elements (from `brand-strategy.md` Section 4 — Visual Identity):**
- Wordmark: "Terrified **Toys**" — Toys in Terror Red `#e8392f`, Terrified in Bone `#f4ead8`, on Void `#0d0d0e` background
- The "It knew" rip-tag motif, if it fits the packaging format
- SKU name per design ("The Deer" / "The Rabbit" / "The Possum")

## Packaging Copy — Tone & Style

Same voice rules as everywhere else (`brand-strategy.md` Section 2) — deadpan, no exclamation points, the expression is the hook, the red internal-stuffing detail is a minor secondary detail, never branded as "gore":

**Front copy (short, punchy):**
> It knew.

**Back copy (slightly longer, the actual sell):**
> A genuinely good chew toy with a face that knows exactly what's coming. Built to survive a few rounds before it doesn't. The destruction is the whole point.

**Care instructions (functional, not just decorative):**
> Supervise play. Remove and discard if damaged. Hand wash only.

**Don't:** use exclamation points, "Fun for the whole family!"-style genericism, or any blood/gore-adjacent language on pack — see `canva-copy.md` for the same copy adapted to digital formats.

## Reference Examples (for visual direction, not to copy directly)

Worth looking at how other novelty/gag-gift pet brands handle packaging restraint — the joke should read from the product itself, not require packaging to over-explain it. Specifically worth studying:
- Any "gag gift" packaging in the gifting/novelty category generally (simple kraft/cardboard, minimal copy, let the product photo/window do the work)
- Avoid: generic big-box pet toy packaging conventions (busy, colourful, multiple call-out bubbles) — that's the opposite of this brand's deadpan restraint
