The backstory

Why New Zealand's rabbits are not your Easter bunny

1838 Year rabbits were first deliberately liberated in New Zealand
1876 Year the first Rabbit Nuisance Act was passed — 38 years after introduction
6 Rabbits can produce up to six litters a year, each with up to eight kits

The European rabbit arrived in New Zealand as a deliberate introduction. Acclimatisation societies of the 1800s believed that importing familiar European fauna would make the new colony feel more like home. The rabbit was considered charming, harmless, and — importantly — edible.

By the 1870s, Otago and Marlborough were overrun. Rabbits don't stop at charming. They graze native tussock down to bare soil, outcompete sheep for pasture, and undermine riverbanks through burrowing. The land that had supported generations of native plants and birds was, within decades, being held in place by essentially nothing. The soil began to move.

The legislative response was extraordinary. New Zealand passed twelve separate pieces of rabbit-related legislation between 1876 and 1956, including the Rabbit Nuisance Act, the Rabbit Act, and eventually the Rabbit and Land Management Programme of the 1990s. Rabbit boards — organisations with the specific mandate of killing rabbits — were established by law across the South Island. At their peak, they employed hundreds of people.

Calicivirus (rabbit haemorrhagic disease) was introduced in the 1990s as a biocontrol agent, causing substantial population crashes. The debate about its ethics was significant. The damage to native ecosystems was more significant.

Today the rabbit is managed but not eradicated — too widespread, too reproductive. What's changed is that New Zealanders have largely stopped pretending the rabbit is a pet. It's a pest. It's on the list. And your dog already knew that.
