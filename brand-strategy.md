# Terrified Toys — Brand Strategy

This is the foundational brand document. `business-plan.md` and `marketing-campaign-plan.md` defer to this for tone, voice, audience, and visual identity rather than redefining them — if something here changes, update it here first, then the other docs link to it.

## 0. Brand Purpose

Terrified Toys exists at the intersection of two true things.

**The joke:** a plush toy with a face frozen in the exact moment of knowing. Your dog destroys it. That's the whole bit.

**The mission:** the possum, rabbit, and deer on these toys are real pest species actively damaging New Zealand's native forests, wildlife, and farmland. Not metaphorically. They're on official control lists. Your dog, by destroying this toy, is participating — symbolically, absurdly, and correctly — in something that actually matters to NZ's ecological future.

The conservation angle does not soften the joke. It sharpens it. The toy's terror is justified. The dog's enthusiasm is vindicated. The destruction was always the right outcome.

**The unifying idea: the destruction is correct.**

Both pillars live in that line. It's funny and true at the same time. Neither one undercuts the other — the comedy makes the conservation point land without lecturing, and the conservation truth makes the joke land harder once you know it.

**Tone rule for the conservation layer:** the brand voice stays deadpan throughout. No earnest eco-warrior register. No "we're committed to protecting NZ's precious biodiversity." The conservation truth should arrive the way the punchline does — something you realize after the initial hit, which makes both the joke funnier and the mission realer. "Your dog is a conservation hero" said with a completely straight face by someone who genuinely means it.

**What this means in practice:**
- The toy page and social ads lead with humour — impulse purchases are not made on ecological guilt
- The conservation layer lives on the packaging hang tag, in a secondary "about the pests" beat on the website, and in captions/copy that reward people who read the whole thing
- Copy should never explain the conservation angle and the joke in the same sentence — let them coexist without being introduced to each other
- "Certified pest species" on packaging is the right register: deadpan, technically accurate, funny in context, real

**On profits/partnerships:** a future option once margin exists is to direct a percentage of sales to Predator Free NZ or a similar official programme — this would be worth stating front-and-centre once it's real. At pre-launch/demo scale, do not imply this before it's confirmed. "Supporting NZ conservation" without a specific commitment is the kind of claim that invites scrutiny.

## 1. Brand Pillars

Every piece of content, copy, or design should trace back to one of these:

1. **The Joke Is Real** — the terror is played completely straight. The toy is not winking at the camera. We never explain the joke; the face does it.
2. **Unapologetically Naughty** — sassy, a little mischievous, comfortable being divisive. We know this won't land with everyone, and that's fine — trying to please everyone makes it less funny to the people it's actually for.
3. **Let The Dogs Sell It** — real pet reactions and user-generated content carry the proof and the laugh, not a founder-origin story. "Watch this dog destroy it" outsells "two people built this in their garage." Founder authenticity is a nice-to-have footnote, not the lead.
4. **Genuinely Good, Not Just A Gag** — it's a real, durable plush. The destruction is earned, not instant. People rebuy because their pet actually loved it, not just for the joke.
5. **The Destruction Is Correct** — these aren't innocent animals; they're documented pest species. The terror on the toy's face is ecologically warranted. The dog doing what comes naturally is, in this specific case, the right outcome. "Good dog" is affection. "Great dog" is a performance review.

## 2. Brand Personality & Voice

**Personality in one line:** sassy, dry, a little feral — the toy knows its fate and so do we.

**Tone attributes:** funny, quirky, naughty, deadpan, warm underneath the edge. Not corporate-cute, not try-hard-edgy. Confident enough to let a joke sit without over-explaining it.

**Do:**
- Play the toy's terror completely straight — the comedy is in the deadpan delivery, not exclamation points.
- Let video/photos carry the punchline; captions support, they don't explain.
- Be a little smug about the destruction ("called it").
- Write like a person texting a friend, not a brand posting an ad.

**Don't:**
- Don't wink at the camera or break character to explain the joke.
- Don't soften copy to avoid mildly offending someone — a few raised eyebrows is the tone working as intended.
- Don't lead with founder/origin-story copy — UGC and product-in-action come first.
- Don't use exclamation-point enthusiasm or generic DTC-brand cheerfulness ("We're SO excited!!").
- **Don't brand the red internal stuffing as "gore," "blood," or a kill/death effect.** It's red fluff that shows through torn seams — a small colour-contrast comedic detail, not a horror feature. The terrified facial expression is the hook; the red stuffing is a minor follow-up beat, never the headline.

**Example lines (in-voice):**

*Humour register:*
- CTA: "Reserve Mine — My Dog Doesn't Need To Know Yet"
- CTA: "Get In Before It Knows You're Coming"
- Email subject: "It's heard about you."
- Caption: "Day 1 of giving him a toy that knows its fate."
- Caption: "She didn't ask for this. He bought it anyway."
- Product copy: "Survived approximately 4 minutes."

*Conservation register (deadpan, not earnest):*
- Hero line: "It knew. It was a pest."
- Dog praise: "Good dog. Great dog."
- Caption: "Your dog is a conservation hero. He doesn't know that. Neither does the possum."
- Packaging tag: "Certified pest species."
- Product copy: "Introduced to New Zealand in 1837. Your dog just helped."  *(possum-specific)*
- Caption: "It wasn't supposed to be here. Your dog understood the assignment."
- Supporting line: "Doing its bit for New Zealand, one toy at a time."
- Deadpan tooltip: "The face is accurate. It knows what it did to the rimu seedlings."

## 3. Product Designs

Three canonical designs, finalized as a brief (sent to manufacturers — see `outreach-emails.md`), but no artwork files exist yet:

1. **NZ deer (stag)** — antlers, the "classic" startled-deer look
   - *Conservation story:* Introduced deer (red, fallow, sika) are significant pests of NZ native forest — they browse regeneration and prevent native bush recovery. Actively managed by DOC and private hunters. The "Hunting Lad" persona overlap is not coincidental: the dog doing what a hunting dog does is the same impulse as pest management by rifle.
   - *Copy angle:* "It went for the seedlings. Got the dog instead." / "Pest control. Good boy." — leans into the hunting-dog register.
   - *Strongest for:* dual humour + conservation story; maps directly to the Hunting Lad persona; the stag expression reads as the most "dignified animal in an undignified moment" which has its own comic register.

2. **Rabbit** — long ears, wide panicked eyes
   - *Conservation story:* Rabbits are established agricultural and ecological pests in NZ and AU — warren systems destroy pasture and soil stability; they've driven localised extinctions of native ground-nesting species. Widespread, officially managed, and everyone knows it.
   - *Copy angle:* leans more into humour — the rabbit's visual is the most legible and the most universally funny (floppy ears + sheer terror = immediately readable). Conservation note plays as a secondary "actually, yes" beat rather than the lead.
   - *Strongest for:* pure humour story; widest audience legibility; the "prank gifter" and "petty partner" personas connect here most easily. The conservation layer adds depth but doesn't need to lead.

3. **Possum** — NZ/AU possum styling
   - *Conservation story:* **The strongest conservation story of the three.** Australian brushtail possums were introduced to NZ in 1837 for the fur trade and became one of the country's most destructive pests — they eat native birds' eggs and chicks, strip native trees of leaves and flowers, and are vectors for bovine TB. NZ kills an estimated 30 million possums annually. The Predator Free 2050 initiative (NZ's national biodiversity goal) lists possums as a primary target. Possum fur products are actively marketed as eco-friendly *because killing them is correct*. There is no ambiguity: this animal is not supposed to be here.
   - *Copy angle:* "Introduced to New Zealand in 1837. Your dog is helping." / "It knew it didn't belong here." / "NZ's most wanted. Your dog just helped." — the conservation angle works as the primary frame here because it's so well-known in NZ/AU culture that it doesn't need explanation.
   - *Strongest for:* conservation story by a wide margin; also strong humour story (possums have a grotesque-cute visual register that lends itself to deadpan). NZ/AU audiences will immediately clock the pest angle.

**Design-level copy strategy summary:**
- Possum: lead with conservation, humour follows — "It knew it didn't belong here" works as both joke and true statement
- Rabbit: lead with humour, conservation as secondary depth — widest audience, keep it light
- Deer/stag: lead with either depending on persona — hunting-adjacent audiences get the pest angle first; general audiences get the expression first

**The terrified facial expression is the main hook on every design** — wide eyes, raised brows, mouth open mid-scream. Each toy also has red internal stuffing, so when the dog chews through the seams, red fluff shows through underneath — a small, secondary comedic detail. **This is not marketed as a "gore" product**: no blood, no graphic framing, nothing that reads as actually disturbing. It's a colour-contrast detail (red fluff peeking through), not a horror effect — the face does the comedic work, the red stuffing is just a fun "oh, there it is" follow-up beat.

The two reference photos currently used on the live pages (`images/black-poodle-deer.png`, `images/apricot-poodle-rabbit.png`) show the expression/tone only — the red stuffing detail isn't visible yet, since real artwork hasn't been produced. Update this section and the live pages once real product photography exists.

## 4. Visual Identity

Formalized from what's already live on `pre-launch.html` — this is the locked identity, not a placeholder.

**Palette:**
| Name | Hex | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| Void (background) | `#0d0d0e` | Primary background |
| Panel (soft background) | `#161616` | Alternating section background |
| Terror Red (accent) | `#e8392f` | CTAs, urgency, accent text only — never body copy |
| Terror Red (dark) | `#b9261d` | Hover/active states |
| Bone (text) | `#f4ead8` | Primary text on dark background |
| Ash (muted text) | `#9a9a9a` | Secondary/supporting text |

**Typography:** current pages use a system sans-serif fallback stack only (`-apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", Helvetica, Arial`). This stays as the body font (clean, fast, no external dependency). Headlines get a single added display font for character — a bold, slightly condensed sans (e.g. via one lightweight Google Fonts import) reserved for `h1`/`h2` only, so the page stays fast and self-contained if the font fails to load (system sans is the fallback either way).

**Photography style:** raw, real pet photos — not stock, not overly staged. The mess and motion blur is part of the brand, not a flaw to retouch out.

**Recurring visual motif:** the "It knew" rip-tag badge (small rotated label on product photos) — reuse this across persona pages and future product photography as a consistent brand element.

**Logo/lockup (interim standard):** wordmark "Terrified **Toys**" — "Toys" in Terror Red, "Terrified" in Bone, uppercase, bold, tight letter-spacing. No icon/symbol yet; revisit once there's budget for a proper mark.

## 5. Target Personas

Two are locked from Daniel's direction, two are proposed and should be validated (or cut) once reviewed.

### Locked

**The Petty Partner** — buys the toy as a gift for a partner's dog, with a wink of relationship mischief ("this'll annoy them, perfect"). Not necessarily a dog person themselves — the appeal is the bit and the banter, not the toy. Skews 25-45, any gender. Trigger: relationship in-jokes, "I saw this and thought of you (and your dog)." Found via: Instagram/Facebook, often around gift-occasion timing (birthdays, anniversaries, "just because" pettiness). Objection to pre-empt: "is this actually a good toy or just a prank prop?" — answer with the durability proof point.

**The Hunting Lad** — owns a working/hunting dog (lab, pointer, similar), outdoors/blokey culture, dry rather than loud humor. The dog is part of his identity and social content. Skews male, 20-45. Trigger: "my dog's already destroyed everything else I've thrown at it, this one at least knows it's coming." Found via: Facebook hunting/dog-breed groups, some Instagram. Objection to pre-empt: durability/quality — this audience is skeptical of gimmick toys; the "genuinely good plush" pillar matters most here.

### Proposed (validate before building out)

**The Dark-Humor Dog-Content Degenerate** *(MailerLite group: "Dog Content")* — heavy TikTok/Reels user embedded in dog-meme and destruction-compilation content. Shares for the bit, buys impulsively off a single video, may not have planned to buy anything. Skews younger, 20-32. Trigger: a single piece of content landing on their feed — pure impulse, near-zero consideration time. Found via: TikTok FYP, Reels. This is the persona most likely to produce the "one breakout video" goal already in the campaign plan — highest viral upside, lower guaranteed spend per person.

**The Prank Gifter** — buys for white elephant, Secret Santa, or group gift exchanges. Doesn't need to own a dog at all — buying for someone else's, or for the joke itself. Motivated by "funniest gift in the group" status. Seasonal spikes: Christmas, white elephant season, "new puppy" gifting. Found via: Instagram/Facebook gift-search intent ("funny gift for dog owner"). Objection to pre-empt: needs the gifting angle to be obvious and fast to grasp from a single image/caption, since this buyer isn't browsing the dog-product category by default.

## 8. Per-Persona Funnels & Landing Pages

Each persona gets a dedicated landing page rather than all channels pointing at one generic page:

- `landing/petty-partner.html`, `landing/hunting-lad.html`, `landing/dog-content.html`, `landing/prank-gifter.html` — same visual system as `pre-launch.html`, persona-specific hero headline, image/caption selection, and CTA copy drawn from the table above.
- **Funnel:** persona's primary channel → their dedicated landing page → MailerLite signup tagged into a persona-specific group (so the email warm-up sequence can speak to that persona specifically) → shared Stripe Payment Link at checkout (checkout itself doesn't fragment, only top-of-funnel messaging does).
- `pre-launch.html` remains the generic/default page for situations where persona isn't known (e.g. warm-buyer outreach, a general bio link).
