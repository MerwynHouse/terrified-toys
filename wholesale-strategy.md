# Terrified Toys — Wholesale & Retail Strategy (NZ/AU)

Status: first draft, written pre-launch with no manufacturer quote and no sales history. All numbers are illustrative until a real cost-of-goods exists. This document assumes the current price point of $30–45 NZD RRP (see `business-plan.md`).

---

## 1. The headline recommendation

**Don't pursue wholesale yet. Stay DTC until the demo run and first 50–100 cold-audience sales validate the product.** Wholesale is a Year 2+ move (see `growth-plan.md`), not a launch move. The rest of this document is the research to have ready when that time comes — not a list of things to do this month.

Reasons, in short:
- We have no landed cost of goods yet (no manufacturer quote). You cannot quote a trade price you don't know you can profitably sell at.
- Retail buyers want proof of sell-through (DTC sales data, social engagement, repeat purchase rate) before they'll take a meeting with an unknown brand. Right now we have a waitlist, not sales.
- DTC margin is far better per unit than wholesale margin (see Section 5). Every dollar of demand we can fill ourselves before going to wholesale is a dollar we keep.

## 2. NZ/AU pet retail chains

### Animates (NZ)
- Contact: business/supplier enquiries go through the [Contact Us](https://www.animates.co.nz/contact-us) page, which has an "Enquire Online" form with a **"Supplier Enquiry"** dropdown option, or email customerservices@animates.co.nz.
- No public self-serve supplier portal or published wholesale terms — this is a relationship-driven, buyer-assessed process, not a form-and-forget application.
- Fit assessment: Animates is NZ's largest pet retail chain (40+ stores) and skews toward established, branded, often international suppliers with national supply capability (multi-store fulfillment, barcoding, EDI-capable invoicing). A single-SKU startup with no manufacturing partner yet is a very weak pitch here. Realistic target only after 1–2 SKUs are proven sellers with real production capacity behind them — likely a Year 2/3 conversation, not Year 1.

### Petstock (NZ and AU)
- NZ contact: [Contact Us](https://www.petstock.co.nz/pages/contact-us) / support ticket system at support.petstock.co.nz — no dedicated supplier page found; this would need a direct enquiry through general contact channels.
- AU: Petstock AU's supplier-facing systems reference EDI compliance (via SPS Commerce), indicating they expect established vendor capability (electronic invoicing, barcoding, ongoing stock management) even from smaller suppliers.
- Fit: Same profile as Animates — large chain, prefers proven suppliers with logistics maturity. Not a Year 1 target.

### Petbarn (AU)
- Contact: **suppliers@petbarn.com.au** — this is a published, direct supplier-enquiry email (the most explicit contact we found across all NZ/AU pet chains).
- Petbarn describes itself as working with a "handpicked" set of quality suppliers — implying a curated, selective process rather than an open application, but the existence of a dedicated supplier inbox is a genuinely good sign that they do entertain new-brand pitches.
- Fit: Best-documented entry point of the big chains. Worth a cold pitch once there's a sell-through track record (Section 6) — but Australia adds shipping/duty/currency complexity, so this is realistically a Year 2+ move after AU DTC demand is proven (per `growth-plan.md` Year 2 plan).

### Pet World (NZ)
- thepetworld.co.nz exists but we found no published supplier/wholesale contact page — likely a smaller independent chain or franchise-style operation. Would require direct outreach to head office or individual store owners; this is not a unified national buying decision the way Animates/Petstock are.
- Fit: Lower priority — smaller footprint, harder to find a single decision-maker, but also likely lower bar to entry for an indie brand. Worth a phone call once we have stock, not worth chasing pre-launch.

**General pattern across all four chains**: none of them advertise an "indie/startup welcome" program. All of them are buyer-gatekept — there's no NZ/AU pet chain equivalent of a self-serve vendor marketplace. You get in by pitching a category buyer directly, and what gets you a meeting is sell-through proof, not a good story.

## 3. Gift and novelty retailers

### Whitcoulls / Paper Plus
- No published supplier/wholesale enquiry page found for either. Paper Plus is structured as a **co-operative franchise** (each store independently owned), which means there is no single national buying decision the way there is at a corporate chain — you'd need to pitch the co-op's central buying group, or individual store owners, separately. Whitcoulls is corporately owned (under the same group) and would have a more centralized buying process, but no public supplier contact was found.
- Fit: Both stock toys and novelty/gift items, which is a plausible category fit for a "prank gift" plush. But getting in the door requires a warm contact or a trade show appearance — there's no cold-email funnel visible from outside.

### The Warehouse (NZ)
- Has the most formal, fully documented supplier process of anything researched: [Becoming a Supplier](https://www.thewarehouse.co.nz/suppliers-be-a-supplier) page lays out the steps explicitly:
  1. Read and agree to their **General Terms of Trade**.
  2. Understand and agree to **Pre-Qualification requirements** (ethical sourcing / factory standards — applies to whoever manufactures the product, not just to us as the brand).
  3. Download and complete the **New Supplier Submission Form**, then email it to their supplier team.
  4. A Buyer or Category Manager assesses the product for range/customer fit. They state they'll "endeavour to reply to all completed submissions" — no guaranteed timeline.
- Fit: This is a mass-market, high-volume retailer. They will want pricing and capacity to support potentially hundreds of stores at once — a real risk for a brand still on a single small-batch manufacturer. Worth keeping the submission form on file for when (if) production scales, but not a near-term target; rejection risk is high if we can't promise consistent volume.

### Typo (Cotton On Group)
- Vendor/supplier/production enquiries go to **production@cottonon.com.au** (confirmed via their own help center). Cotton On Group designs centrally and outsources production to ~850 factories — meaning Typo isn't looking for "finished branded product" suppliers in the way a pet store is; they're more likely to want to manufacture their own version of an idea than to wholesale-stock someone else's branded product. This channel is a weaker fit than it first appears.
- Fit: Low priority. Typo's model doesn't naturally support stocking an outside brand's product line; it's built around developing its own gift/stationery SKUs through its supplier network. Not worth pursuing unless told otherwise by someone inside the business.

**Pattern**: gift/novelty retail is harder to break into cold than pet retail — less transparency, more co-op/franchise structures, and (in Typo's case) a manufacturing model that doesn't fit a "stock our brand" pitch at all.

## 4. Online marketplaces

### Trade Me (NZ)
- As of March 2026, Trade Me **removed the 7.9% success fee for casual sellers**. General item listings are free within a 50-listing/month allowance (then $0.10 per extra listing). If a buyer pays via Ping, there's a 2.19% transaction fee on the seller side.
- **Professional/"in-trade" sellers** (which a registered business selling new stock at volume would be classified as) still pay success fees and operate under a different, ongoing commercial arrangement with Trade Me — the casual-seller fee removal doesn't apply to us as a business.
- Fit: Good low-friction channel to test market demand outside the existing waitlist/social funnel, and NZ buyers already trust and browse it daily. Realistic as a secondary sales channel once stock exists — lower priority than the Shopify-deferred DTC site, but worth a test listing in parallel with the demo run since the marginal cost is near zero.

### Mighty Ape (NZ/AU)
- Mighty Ape Marketplace takes roughly a **9% commission** per sale; the company handles payment escrow and ships direct from the seller to the customer.
- Mighty Ape's customer base is skewed toward games/entertainment/pop-culture products — there's crossover with our "novelty gift" persona (the Prank Gifter) but it's not a pet-specific audience. Worth testing as a secondary channel, not a primary one.

### Catch.com.au (AU)
- Catch is a **curated marketplace**: sellers submit an application, Catch reviews it, and if approved a Catch rep contacts you to onboard. There's a **$49.99/month (incl. GST) subscription fee** (waived for the first 2 months), plus a **category commission of 8–20%** on top.
- The combination of a monthly subscription + a meaningful commission makes Catch a real cost commitment, not a free test channel — better suited to a moment when AU demand is already proven (i.e., after DTC sales show AU buyers exist) rather than as a discovery channel.

**Marketplace recommendation**: Trade Me is the only one worth testing immediately and cheaply, given near-zero seller fees for a small volume of new-stock listings. Mighty Ape and Catch are credible Year 2 AU/secondary-channel additions once volume justifies the commission/subscription cost — don't lead with them.

## 5. What trade pricing looks like at $30–40 NZD RRP

Industry-standard pet retail wholesale discount is **40–50% off RRP** (i.e., the retailer buys at roughly half of what they sell it for — "keystone" pricing, where the retailer doubles their buy price to set RRP). Toys specifically are a healthier-margin category for retailers than food/commodity pet products, so chains will expect to be *at least* at that 50% discount level, not less.

At our price point, illustrative trade structure:

| RRP | Trade price to retailer (50% off) | What we receive per unit |
|---|---|---|
| $30 NZD | $15 NZD | $15 NZD |
| $35 NZD | $17.50 NZD | $17.50 NZD |
| $40 NZD | $20 NZD | $20 NZD |

That $15–20 NZD per unit then has to cover landed cost of goods, freight to the retailer's warehouse/DC, any co-op marketing fees the chain charges, and our own margin — and we don't yet know our cost of goods (no manufacturer quote, per `business-plan.md`). **Until that number exists, we cannot responsibly commit to a wholesale price**, because if landed cost turns out to be anywhere near $10–12/unit, a 50%-off trade price leaves almost nothing after freight and chain fees.

Compare to DTC: at $30–40 direct, after payment processing (~3%) and our own shipping cost, we keep most of the margin ourselves. Wholesale roughly **halves per-unit revenue** in exchange for volume and distribution we don't have to generate ourselves through content/ads. That trade-off only makes sense once organic/paid DTC acquisition is maxing out or too slow relative to demand.

## 6. When to pursue wholesale vs. stay DTC

**Stay DTC while:**
- Cost of goods isn't locked in with a manufacturer (current state).
- Unit volume is below what justifies the lower per-unit margin — wholesale only "pays" once volume from a chain meaningfully exceeds what we could generate ourselves at full price in the same period.
- We don't yet have sell-through data (units sold, repeat-purchase rate, social engagement) to put in front of a buyer. Buyers want proof, not a pitch deck.

**Consider wholesale once:**
- Demo run + first 50–100 cold-audience DTC sales are sold and validated (per `growth-plan.md` Year 1 targets).
- Cost of goods is known and stable across at least one production run, so a trade price can be set without guessing.
- We have 2+ SKUs (the current 3-design lineup gives us this) — single-SKU pitches are weak; buyers want a small range to fill shelf space.
- DTC organic reach is plateauing and a chain's existing foot traffic represents genuinely incremental customers we can't reach ourselves (rather than cannibalizing direct sales we'd have gotten anyway).

**Rough revenue/volume threshold**: don't approach a chain buyer until you can show consistent sell-through of 200+ units/month sustained for at least a full quarter through DTC. That's the kind of evidence that turns "no" into "let's talk," and it roughly lines up with the Year 2 AU-expansion stage already laid out in `growth-plan.md`.

## 7. How to approach a buyer cold — what works, what gets you rejected

**What buyers want to see (in rough order of weight):**
1. **Proof of demand** — actual sales numbers, not projections. Screenshots of sell-through, repeat purchase rate, social engagement/reach on the product specifically (not just brand follower count).
2. **Supply reliability** — a real manufacturer relationship, confirmed lead times, and the ability to fulfil a wholesale order size (which is bigger and more sudden than DTC trickle demand) without blowing out timelines.
3. **Margin headroom for them** — a trade price that leaves them their expected 40–50%+ margin after their own costs. Coming in asking for a price that barely works for *us* and leaves no room for *them* is an instant pass.
4. **Brand differentiation that fits their shelf** — a clear one-line reason this isn't a duplicate of something already in their range. Our "terrified plush" hook is genuinely distinctive in a chain's toy aisle — lead with that, not with backstory.
5. **Compliance basics in order** — barcodes/GS1 NZ numbers, product liability insurance, and (per `packaging-brief.md`) clean packaging with proper labeling. Buyers will ask for these early; not having them ready signals "not retail-ready yet."

**What gets you rejected:**
- Pitching before you have sales data — "we think people will love this" instead of "here's what people are already buying."
- Asking for a trade price that doesn't leave the standard 40–50% margin — buyers won't negotiate this down, they'll just say no.
- Vague or no commitment on supply timelines/minimum order quantities — a buyer who orders 500 units and gets told "8–12 weeks, maybe" will not reorder.
- No packaging/compliance readiness — turning up to a buyer meeting with a product but no barcode, no safety/labeling info, and no clear case pack size reads as unready, regardless of how good the product itself is.
- Leading with story/mission instead of numbers. Buyers are evaluating shelf-space ROI, not brand passion — save the story for the actual product packaging and marketing, not the pitch.

**Practical first move when the time comes**: Petbarn's published supplier email (suppliers@petbarn.com.au) and The Warehouse's formal submission form are the two most concrete, documented entry points found in this research — both are good templates for the kind of cold pitch packet to prepare (sell-through data, trade pricing, supply capacity, compliance docs) even if Animates/Petstock end up being warmer relationship-based approaches instead.

## Summary

| Channel | Near-term priority | Why |
|---|---|---|
| DTC (own site/social) | **Primary, now** | Best margin, no gatekeeper, builds the sales proof every other channel requires |
| Trade Me | Low-cost secondary test | Near-zero fees for new-stock listings, good NZ reach |
| Animates / Petstock | Year 2+ | Buyer-gatekept, want supply maturity we don't have yet |
| Petbarn (AU) | Year 2+ | Best-documented contact, but AU complexity = wait for AU DTC proof first |
| Pet World | Opportunistic, low effort | Smaller chain, no clear national process, worth a call once stock exists |
| Whitcoulls/Paper Plus | Low priority | No clear cold-entry path; co-op structure complicates buying decision |
| The Warehouse | Keep on file | Most formal process, but high-volume expectations are a stretch this early |
| Typo | Deprioritize | Manufacturing model doesn't fit a "stock our brand" pitch |
| Mighty Ape / Catch | Year 2 AU secondary | Real fees/commission make them a scale-stage move, not a discovery channel |

**Bottom line: don't build a wholesale pitch yet — build the DTC sales numbers that will make the pitch land later.**
